Cloud GPUs for serious AI training

Your Apple Silicon Mac is a genuinely capable AI machine — for running models and light fine-tuning. For heavy training and distillation, you rent a different chip for a few hours. Here's the honest map.

What runs great on your Mac

Apple Silicon uses a technology called MPS (Metal) for AI math. The ml-lab setup installs only what truly works here:

  • Running models locally — Ollama, llama.cpp, and LM Studio for chat and inference.
  • PyTorch (MPS) and the full Hugging Face stack for experiments and inference.
  • MLX & mlx-lm — Apple's own framework for native fine-tuning and LoRA (lightweight fine-tuning) right on your Mac.
  • JupyterLab for notebooks.

For most people learning AI, that's plenty — you can fine-tune small/medium models with MLX without ever leaving your desk.

What needs an NVIDIA (CUDA) GPU

Some tools are built specifically for NVIDIA's CUDA platform and simply error on a Mac. We deliberately do not install these locally:

ToolWhat it's forWhy not on Mac
unsloth2–5× faster fine-tuningCUDA-only kernels
bitsandbytes4/8-bit quantized trainingCUDA-only (no MPS build)
axolotl (CUDA paths)full training pipelinesassumes NVIDIA GPUs

Big jobs — training a model from scratch, full fine-tunes of large models, distillation — want lots of fast GPU memory (an NVIDIA A100/H100 or similar). You don't buy that; you rent it by the hour.

The cloud-GPU path

  1. Rent an NVIDIA box

    Providers like RunPod, Lambda, Vast.ai, or the big clouds (AWS/GCP/Azure) rent GPU machines by the hour. Pick a recent NVIDIA GPU (e.g. A100/H100) and a PyTorch + CUDA image.

  2. Get in

    You connect over SSH or a hosted Jupyter notebook — the same tools you already use. Ask Claude Code or Codex: “help me connect to my RunPod box and set up training.”

  3. Move what transfers

    Transfers cleanly: your code, your dataset, your Hugging Face models, your training scripts — they're identical on Mac and Linux/CUDA. Doesn't transfer: anything MPS-specific or MLX (those are Apple-only; on CUDA you use the standard PyTorch/CUDA path instead).

  4. Train, then bring results home

    Run the job on the rented GPU, then download the resulting model (or push it to the Hugging Face Hub). Stop the box when done so you stop paying — you only rent it for the hours you train.

💸

Develop and debug on small data locally (free), then rent the big GPU only for the final full run. It keeps costs to a few dollars.

A good rule of thumb

Prototype on your Mac, scale in the cloud. Learn, experiment, and fine-tune small models here with MLX; rent an NVIDIA GPU only when a job genuinely needs it. Either way, your assistants can drive the whole thing — just describe what you're trying to train.
